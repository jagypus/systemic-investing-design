# Layer 1 — Intent, Positionality & Paradigm

Sources: TransCap Initiative (Dominic Hofstetter and colleagues — white paper and "What is systemic investing?"), TWIST (Together We Invest for Systems Transformation — Korijn & Fort with the TWIST community of practice, 2024), the Investor's Guide to Systemic Investing (CSP/UZH, MIT Sloan, TransCap, The ImPact, TWIST, 2025), TIIP / Burckart & Lydenberg (*21st Century Investing*).

## What systemic investing is

The deployment of financial capital to transform human and natural systems — advancing environmental sustainability and social justice — by funding *coordinated combinations* of interventions rather than single-point solutions. Its defining move is directional:

- **Conventional/impact logic (capital → system):** start with the capital you have and its return requirements; find investments that also do good.
- **Systemic logic (system → capital):** start with the system you intend to help transform; work out what combination of shifts it needs; derive what kinds of money (and non-money) those shifts require; *then* structure capital to match.

Hallmarks: transformational intent; a systems mindset in problem framing; a systemic theory of transformation guiding interventions; strategic multi-asset portfolios designed for combinatorial ("1+1=3") effects; coordination with non-financial actors; and measurement aimed at system health rather than end-of-causal-chain outputs.

## The lineage map (use for vocabulary discipline)

| Term | Lineage | Centre of gravity | When it's the right frame |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Systemic investing** | TransCap Initiative; TWIST; CSP/MIT Investor's Guide | Wealth holders, foundations, catalytic funders deploying *multiple kinds of capital* into a specific system (place, value chain) | The user wants to transform a nameable system and can flex across the capital spectrum |
| **System-level investing** | TIIP / Burckart & Lydenberg | Institutional investors (pensions, insurers) managing *systemic risk* across whole portfolios; beta stewardship, field-building, policy voice | The user is a fiduciary institution whose main lever is how it holds and stewards conventional assets |
| **Systems change investing / portfolio approaches** | Umbrella: UNDP portfolio guidebook, Tamarack, SSIR literature | Funders and public agencies organizing initiatives (often grants) as portfolios | The user is primarily a grantmaker or public funder |
| **Impact investing** | GIIN lineage | Deal-level intentionality + measurable impact + (usually) financial return | The user wants good deals, not system transformation — a legitimate choice; name it as such |

Most users arrive speaking "impact investing." Don't correct the vocabulary; surface the directional question (system→capital or capital→system?) and let them locate themselves.

## The wealth-holder journey (TWIST)

For private wealth holders and family offices, systemic investing is identity work before it is portfolio work. The TWIST community's practice insights:

- **The portfolio is one lever among several.** A wealth holder also has voice (public positions, shareholder voice), networks (convening power, social capital), political capital, consumption and procurement, and the story their wealth tells. A systemic strategy deploys several levers, not just AUM.
- **Peer learning beats solo pioneering.** The field is young; communities of practice (TWIST and peers) compress the learning curve and pool deal/intervention flow. For smaller holders, joining an existing strategic portfolio is usually higher-leverage than building one.
- **Expect the inner shift.** Moving from "what return does my impact make" to "what does the system need from me" reorders the family's relationship to control, time horizon, and credit-taking. Treat resistance as material to work with, not an obstacle.

## Positionality: the investor inside the system

Before any mapping or structuring, establish honestly:

1. **Mandate and constraints** — whose money is this legally; what can it actually do (fiduciary duty, foundation payout rules, family governance)? Note: fiduciary-duty *reframing* is itself a recognized leverage point in the field — constraints are sometimes interpretations, not laws. Flag, don't assume.
2. **Origin of the capital** — what created this wealth, and does that history implicate the very system being targeted? (Not a guilt exercise — a design input: it shapes trust, legitimacy, and which role the investor can credibly play.)
3. **Power and decision rights** — who decides allocations, and how will people living in the target system get real decision power, not consultation theater?
4. **Time horizon and exit psychology** — transformation runs decades; what happens at generational transition, board turnover, or the first bad evaluation?
5. **Role self-assessment** — capital provider, orchestrator/backbone, convener, advocate, or follower of others' strategies? Most allocators overestimate their orchestration capacity (see scale honesty, SKILL.md).

## The portfolio-metaphor tension (name it, don't resolve it)

Tamarack (Cabaj, Wellsch & Perla) argues social innovators should *drop* the business-portfolio metaphor: systems-change portfolios exist to shift patterns through mutually reinforcing initiatives, not to diversify risk and optimize return per asset; standardized metrics across a mixed portfolio misrepresent it. TransCap *builds on* investment language: strategic portfolios, asset classes, instruments. Both are right in their domain. Practical rule: **financial-portfolio logic for structuring the instruments; systems-portfolio logic for selecting and evaluating the interventions.** Present this as one of the field's creative tensions — the user will meet both cultures and should be able to translate.

## Goal-state framing

The field's stated goal is regenerative systems — ecological and social health that renews itself (Fullerton's regenerative-finance principles are the common articulation). Anchor intent statements there rather than in deficit language ("less harm", "closing gaps") — the difference cascades into every later layer.
