# Layer 4 — Capital Architecture

Sources: TransCap (strategic/nested portfolios, financial backbones, full capital spectrum), catalytic capital practice (MacArthur C3, Tideline), blended-finance practice (Convergence), Mazzucato (public capital as co-investor), Patton Power (*Adventure Finance* — instrument design; see also the sibling regenerative-business-design skill's finance reference for venture-side instruments).

Core idea: derive the capital from the intervention strategy, not vice versa. Each intervention in the layer-3 set has its own capital physics (risk, tenor, return capacity, fundability); the architecture's job is to give each the *right kind of money* while keeping the whole governed as one strategy.

## The full capital spectrum

Systemic investing's working definition of "capital" is deliberately wide. Inventory what the strategy can deploy or mobilize:

| Capital type | Typical role in a systemic portfolio |
|---|---|
| Market-rate investment (equity, debt, real assets) | Scale-stage interventions with standalone economics |
| Concessional/catalytic capital (below-market debt/equity, guarantees, first-loss) | De-risk and crowd in commercial capital; hold the riskiest load-bearing positions |
| Philanthropic grants | Non-investable levers (policy, narrative, organizing), probes, backbone & learning infrastructure, community capacity |
| Recoverable/convertible grants | Pre-commercial interventions with a path to revenue |
| Public finance (procurement, subsidies, taxes, guarantees, mission funds) | Rule-level shifts; demand creation; scale beyond private capacity |
| Corporate finance (supply-chain finance, offtakes, commitments) | Value-chain boundaries; demand signals |
| Insurance & risk instruments | Resilience interventions; unlocking other capital by capping tail risk |
| Outcome-based instruments (impact bonds, outcomes funds, credits) | Where a payer exists for verified system outcomes — integrity caution on credits |
| Community capital (community shares, DPOs, member capital) | Legitimacy and local ownership as much as money |

Two structural rules of thumb:
- **Grants are load-bearing, not garnish.** In most systemic portfolios the grant-funded components (backbone, learning, policy, organizing) carry the thesis; size them accordingly (often 10–30% of total deployment, not 1–2%).
- **Catalytic positions should have a crowding-in story.** First-loss and guarantees exist to change other investors' math; if no other capital is expected to follow, the instrument is just a quiet subsidy — sometimes fine, but name it.

## Strategic (nested) portfolio structure

The TransCap pattern: a multi-asset-class portfolio *nested inside* the broader intervention strategy — investments selected and governed for combinatorial effects, sitting alongside non-investable interventions funded by grants and public money.

Design decisions to make explicit:

1. **Vehicle geometry** — single blended vehicle (one fund, tranched: junior/first-loss, mezzanine, senior) vs. **aligned-sleeves** (each funder deploys from its own balance sheet under a shared thesis and governance — lighter, faster, legally simpler, weaker enforceability) vs. **fund-of-positions** anchored by a backbone that arranges deal-by-deal syndication. Most place-based strategies start with aligned sleeves + a backbone and consider a pooled vehicle only once the pipeline proves out.
2. **Sequencing** — probes and capacity grants → catalytic positions in keystone interventions → commercial capital as economics mature → public capital for rule-level scaling. Capital that arrives too early at market-rate terms forces premature commercialization of interventions that needed patience.
3. **Liquidity & tenor honesty** — transformation horizons (10–20y) vs. fund lifetimes (8–12y) vs. family patience (variable). Prefer evergreen structures, long-dated notes, or explicit hand-off plans over pretending a 7-year fund will transform a food system.
4. **The learning budget line** — systemic intelligence, sensemaking, and evaluation funded as portfolio infrastructure (typically 3–7% of deployment), with a named owner (see layer 5).

## The financial backbone

The orchestrating entity of a Synergy-type portfolio: develops and stewards the thesis, originates and structures interventions, aligns capital providers, runs the learning system, holds relationships with the community and non-financial actors. Options: stand one up (expensive — realistic only for large strategies or funder collaboratives), host the function in an existing trusted intermediary (CDFI, community foundation, regional development body), or join a strategy that has one. Fund it properly: under-funded backbones become deal brokers, because transactions are what people will pay for.

## Governance & decision rights

- **One governance for the whole strategy**, spanning instruments — an investment committee for the investables plus a *strategy council* (funders + community + key partners) holding the thesis, the map, and adaptation decisions. If only the IC exists, financial logic will quietly take over.
- **Community decision power located explicitly** (see layer 3) — e.g., community seats with votes on intervention selection; community veto on boundary/thesis changes; participatory grantmaking for the community-capacity sleeve.
- **Pre-agree adaptation rights** — who can move money between sleeves when learning says the thesis was wrong, and how fast. Blended vehicles with rigid tranche mandates can't adapt; build re-allocation windows in.

## The honest downside ledger

Present these costs explicitly whenever recommending a systemic architecture — allocators deserve the skeptical-CIO comparison, not a pitch:

- **Coordination overhead is real and permanent.** Multi-funder, multi-instrument strategies spend serious money and senior time on alignment; expect materially higher cost-per-deployed-dollar than a conventional fund. If the combinatorial thesis is weak, this overhead buys nothing.
- **Track record is thin.** Few completed cycles exist; benchmarks, exemplary case law, and experienced staff are scarce; most named precedents are mid-journey. Honesty: this is a frontier practice, partly built in flight.
- **Attribution will frustrate boards.** System-health evidence is slower and softer than IRR; a board that demands quarterly attribution will unravel the strategy (layer 5 must be designed in, and the board signed up, *before* deployment).
- **Fiduciary and regulatory friction.** Blended structures, cross-subsidy between tranches, and non-financial decision rights raise genuine legal questions that vary by jurisdiction and entity type — budget for specialist counsel; some designs won't survive it.
- **Illiquidity and long lock-ups** compound family/board turnover risk; an exit of one anchor funder can break a strategy mid-arc — pre-negotiate succession and wind-down.
- **Systems-washing risk runs both ways.** An ordinary deal pipeline can dress itself in systems language; equally, a genuinely systemic strategy can be pressured into reporting itself as a deal pipeline. The architecture (governance, metrics, budget lines) is what holds the difference.
- **When simpler wins.** If the user's capital is small, staff thin, or the system already has a capable orchestrator — joining, co-funding, or a single well-placed catalytic cheque beats architecting. If the user mainly wants reliable impact deals, honest impact investing serves them better than a systemic costume. Say so.
