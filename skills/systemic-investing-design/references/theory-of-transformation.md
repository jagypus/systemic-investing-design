# Layer 3 — Theory of Transformation & Intervention Strategy

Sources: TransCap (combinatorial change, systemic theory of transformation), Tamarack Institute — Cabaj, Wellsch & Perla, *There is No Such Thing as a "Fish"* (portfolio typology), Mazzucato (mission-oriented coordination), Climate-KIC deep-demonstration practice.

Core idea: a theory of *change* says how one intervention causes one outcome; a theory of *transformation* says which **combination of shifts, in what relation to each other**, would move the system — and therefore what mix of interventions (investable and not) the strategy must hold. Transformation results from multiple shifts happening with strategic coherence, not from any single solution scaled hard ("no silver bullets — maybe silver buckshot").

## Building the combinatorial thesis

From the layer-2 map, draft:

1. **The shifts** — 3–6 changes that together would re-pattern the system, spread across depths (a resource-flow shift alone won't hold without a rules or mental-model shift to anchor it — six-conditions check).
2. **The relations** — how shifts reinforce each other and in what sequence (the European Green Deal pattern: carbon pricing *plus* a social compensation fund *plus* skills transition *plus* demand-side regulation — each protects the political and practical feasibility of the others).
3. **The actors** — who is already working on each shift; what they lack (money is only sometimes the answer).
4. **The intervention set** — investable interventions (companies, projects, infrastructure, funds) *and* non-investable levers (policy advocacy, narrative/media work, community organizing, research, convening, litigation). A systemic strategy that funds only investables has delegated its hardest shifts to hope.

Express the thesis falsifiably: "We believe shifts A+B+C, pursued together in [boundary], will weaken loop X and strengthen loop Y — visible within [period] as [system-health signals]." Layer 5 will hold this to account.

## Name the portfolio you're actually designing (Tamarack typology)

"Portfolio" is not one thing. Each archetype has a distinct niche, theory of change, minimum capabilities, and evaluation logic — most real portfolios are hybrids (nested, blended, or evolving across types):

| Archetype | Primary intent | When it fits | Minimum capabilities |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cabinet of Curiosities** | Surface diverse ideas and possibilities | Wide engagement matters; conditions for sharp focus don't yet exist | Ambiguity tolerance; convening; insight harvesting |
| **System Probes** | Learn how the system responds to intervention | System dynamics/leverage points still uncertain (see layer 2 test) | Safe-to-fail experiment design; harvest & sensemaking |
| **Stage Gate** | Surface, test, then scale the most promising solutions | Many candidate solutions need filtering | Structured evaluation machinery; staged funding |
| **Strategic Gaps** | Fill the missing pieces blocking a system response | Well-mapped ecosystem; clear bottlenecks; others active on adjacent pieces | Strong field mapping; periodic gap re-analysis |
| **Risk-Reward** | Balance reliable plays with transformative bets | Pressure for tangible progress + appetite for moonshots | Risk articulation; differentiated support & metrics |
| **Synergy** | Interventions designed to reinforce each other toward system shift | System well understood; partners willing to adapt their work to each other | **Backbone/orchestration capacity**, systems mapping, cross-initiative coordination |

TransCap's "strategic portfolio" is essentially the **Synergy** archetype — the most demanding type. Run the fit test honestly:

- **Type ↔ system knowledge.** Thin layer 2 → start with Probes; evolve toward Strategic Gaps or Synergy as patterns emerge (the evolutionary hybrid is the *normal* maturation path, not a compromise).
- **Type ↔ capability.** Designing a Synergy portfolio without backbone capacity is the most common failure mode in the field. If the capability isn't there, either fund/join a backbone or choose a humbler archetype.
- **Type ↔ expectations.** Each archetype warrants different success measures (layer 5). Expecting direct impact from probes, or uniform KPIs across a mixed portfolio, sets the strategy up to be judged a failure by the wrong test.

In **Mode C diagnostics**, the highest-value finding is usually the archetype mismatch: a Cabinet of Curiosities that believes itself a Synergy portfolio. Deliver it charitably — every archetype has a legitimate niche; the problem is the self-description, the metrics, and the missing capabilities, not the eclecticism.

## Partner ecology and orchestration

- **Map who else funds/acts in the boundary** before designing interventions — a Strategic Gaps logic toward *other funders* avoids duplication and crowding-out. The strategy should state what it deliberately leaves to others.
- **Decide the orchestration arrangement**: a dedicated financial backbone (entity aligning multiple capital providers around the thesis), a hosted backbone function inside an existing intermediary, or distributed coordination via a funder collaborative. Synergy-type portfolios need one of these named, governed, and funded; "we'll coordinate informally" predicts drift back to deal-by-deal logic.
- **Community agency is structural, not consultative** — people living in the system hold real decision rights somewhere in the governance (intervention selection, evaluation sensemaking, veto on boundary changes). Specify where, or say honestly that they don't and treat it as a named tension.
- **Non-investable levers need owners and money.** Policy advocacy and narrative work are usually grant-funded and usually under-resourced relative to their load-bearing role in the thesis. If a shift depends on a lever nobody is resourced to pull, the thesis is decorative.

## Anti-patterns

- **Deal-first retrofitting** — assembling the deals, then drawing a theory of transformation around them (the tell: every "shift" has exactly one investable attached).
- **Coherence theater** — initiatives listed as "mutually reinforcing" with no mechanism by which they actually interact (shared geography is not synergy).
- **Over-orchestration** — forcing synergy where independent excellence would serve; coordination has real costs and partners have their own theories of change.
- **Solo-hero strategy** — a thesis that requires the funder to be everywhere; check against scale honesty and the partner map.
