# Layer 2 — System Understanding

Sources: Donella Meadows (*Thinking in Systems*, "Leverage Points"), Kania/Kramer/Senge (*The Water of Systems Change*), TransCap (systemic intelligence), systems-mapping practice (causal loop diagrams, actor mapping), Snowden/Cynefin (probing under complexity).

Core idea: you cannot derive a capital architecture from a system you haven't understood — and you cannot fully understand a complex system before acting in it. So this layer has two jobs: build the best current map, and build the *ongoing sensing capacity* that keeps the map alive. Both are fundable assets (see layer 4).

## Boundary choice first

The single highest-consequence decision at this layer. Common boundary types:

- **Place** (a region's food system, a river basin, a city's housing system) — strongest for community agency and combinatorial design; the dominant systemic-investing pattern.
- **Value chain / supply chain** (cotton, cocoa, construction materials) — crosses geographies; partners are firms and standard-setters.
- **Sector/regime** (mobility, long-term care, energy) — largest scale; usually requires public co-investors.

Boundary heuristics: small enough that the portfolio's interventions can plausibly interact (combinatorial effects need proximity); large enough to contain the dynamics that hold the problem in place; drawn *with* the people who live there, not just around them. Always record what was excluded and why — excluded dynamics return as "context" risks.

## Mapping the system

Don't aim for the perfect map; aim for a shared, revisable one. Useful methods, in rough order of effort:

1. **Actor & relationship mapping** — who holds money, rules, narrative, and trust; where relationships are missing or extractive.
2. **Causal loop / stock-flow sketching** — the reinforcing loops that hold the status quo and the balancing loops that resist change. Even rough loops discipline the theory of transformation.
3. **Six-conditions scan (Water of Systems Change)** — at three depths: explicit (policies, practices, resource flows), semi-explicit (relationships & connections, power dynamics), implicit (mental models). A portfolio that only touches the explicit layer will be reabsorbed by the implicit one.
4. **Leverage-point scan (Meadows)** — rank candidate intervention sites: parameters < buffers < flows < delays < feedback loops < information flows < rules < self-organization < goals < paradigms. Capital instinctively crowds into parameters (cheapest to fund, weakest to shift); the skill's job is to keep goals/rules/information-flows on the table.
5. **Lived-experience inquiry** — interviews, journey mapping, community sensemaking. Non-optional: the people inside the system hold knowledge no consultant map contains, and their agency is both a means and an end.

## Knowing when you don't know enough

Honest test before advancing to layer 3: can you name (a) 2–3 loops that hold the problem in place, (b) the actors with power over each, (c) at least one leverage point above "parameters", and (d) who in the system validated this picture? If not, the right layer-3 design is a **System Probes portfolio** — small, safe-to-fail, deliberately varied interventions whose purpose is to reveal dynamics, not to deliver impact (Snowden: in complexity, you discover the system's disposition by probing it). Budget real money for this; calling it "pipeline development" misprices it.

## Systemic intelligence as standing infrastructure

One-off mapping decays in months. Design for continuous sensing:

- A named **sensemaking owner** (person or backbone team) who synthesizes signals across the portfolio.
- A **cadence** — quarterly pattern-spotting sessions with investees, community members, and co-funders; annual map revision.
- **Information flows as interventions** — often the cheapest high-leverage move is making the system visible to itself (shared dashboards, field maps, open data). Fund these as first-class portfolio components, not overhead.

## Anti-patterns

- **Map worship** — an 18-month mapping project that exhausts budget and goodwill before any action. Map enough to act; let probes refine it.
- **Helicopter mapping** — consultants map, community validates a slide deck, funder decides. Power analysis done *to* people reproduces the extraction it diagnoses.
- **Boundary gerrymandering** — drawing the system around the deals you already wanted to do. The tell: the map's leverage points all happen to be investable.
- **Static-map planning** — treating the map as settled truth; complex systems move, especially once you intervene in them.
